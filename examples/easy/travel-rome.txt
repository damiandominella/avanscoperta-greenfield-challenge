Rome is the capital city of Italy and one of the most historically rich destinations in the world. Founded according to legend by Romulus and Remus in 753 BC, the city has been a center of power, culture, and religion for over two thousand years. Today it attracts millions of tourists each year who come to walk its ancient streets and experience its unique blend of past and present.

The Colosseum is arguably Rome's most iconic landmark. Originally known as the Flavian Amphitheatre, it was completed in 80 AD and could hold up to 80,000 spectators. Visitors today can explore the underground tunnels where gladiators and wild animals once waited before entering the arena. Booking tickets in advance is highly recommended to avoid the long queues that form every morning.

Just a short walk from the Colosseum lies the Roman Forum, the political and social heart of ancient Rome. Here you can see the ruins of temples, basilicas, and government buildings that once shaped the course of Western civilization. A combined ticket covers both the Colosseum and the Forum, making it easy to visit both sites in a single morning.

The Palatine Hill overlooks the Forum and offers some of the best panoramic views in the city. According to Roman mythology, this is where the she-wolf nursed Romulus and Remus. The hill is covered with the ruins of imperial palaces and beautiful gardens, and it is significantly less crowded than the Colosseum below.

Vatican City is a must-visit even for non-religious travelers. As the smallest independent state in the world, it houses St. Peter's Basilica, the Sistine Chapel, and the vast Vatican Museums. Plan to spend at least half a day here, and consider booking a guided tour to skip the general admission line which can stretch for hours.

The Sistine Chapel, painted by Michelangelo between 1508 and 1512, features one of the most celebrated ceilings in the history of art. The chapel is at the end of the Vatican Museums route, so you will walk through kilometers of galleries before reaching it. Photography is not allowed inside, and guards will ask you to remain silent to preserve the atmosphere.

Trastevere is one of Rome's most charming neighborhoods, known for its narrow cobblestone streets, ivy-covered buildings, and vibrant nightlife. It sits on the west bank of the Tiber River and is the perfect area for an evening stroll. The neighborhood is packed with trattorias, wine bars, and artisan shops that feel far removed from the tourist crowds.

Roman cuisine is simple, bold, and deeply tied to local tradition. The four iconic Roman pasta dishes are carbonara, cacio e pepe, amatriciana, and gricia. Each uses a small number of high-quality ingredients — guanciale, pecorino romano, black pepper, and tomato in various combinations — and mastering them is a point of pride for local chefs.

When eating in Rome, avoid restaurants with large photo menus posted outside, as these typically cater to tourists and serve mediocre food at inflated prices. Instead, look for small trattorias where the menu is handwritten and changes daily. Neighborhoods like Testaccio, Trastevere, and Pigneto are excellent for authentic dining.

Gelato is an essential part of any Roman visit. Look for shops that display gelato in covered metal bins rather than towering colorful mounds, as the latter often indicates artificial ingredients. Some of the best gelaterias in Rome include spots near the Pantheon and in the Monti district, where artisans use fresh seasonal fruit and real pistachios.

The Pantheon is one of the best-preserved ancient Roman buildings and is free to enter. Its massive unreinforced concrete dome, completed around 125 AD, remains the largest of its kind in the world. The oculus at the top is open to the sky, and on rainy days you can watch water fall through it onto the marble floor below.

Piazza Navona is a beautiful baroque square built on the site of an ancient Roman stadium. It features three ornate fountains, the most famous being Bernini's Fountain of the Four Rivers at its center. The piazza is a popular gathering spot surrounded by cafés and street artists, though prices at the terrace restaurants tend to be steep.

The Spanish Steps connect the Piazza di Spagna at the base with the Trinità dei Monti church at the top. They are one of the widest staircases in Europe and a popular spot for sitting and people-watching. Note that since 2019, sitting on the steps is officially prohibited, and fines can be issued to enforce this rule.

Rome's public transport system includes buses, trams, and three metro lines. A single ticket costs a couple of euros and is valid for 100 minutes across all modes of transport. However, many of Rome's best sights are within walking distance of each other in the historic center, so comfortable shoes are often your best investment.

The best time to visit Rome is during spring, from April to June, or in early autumn, September and October. Summer temperatures regularly exceed 35 degrees Celsius, and the city is at peak tourist capacity in July and August. Winter is mild compared to northern Europe and brings the advantage of significantly smaller crowds.

The Appian Way is one of the earliest and most important Roman roads, stretching southeast from the city. Walking or cycling along it takes you past ancient tombs, catacombs, and crumbling aqueducts in a remarkably peaceful countryside setting. It is best visited on Sundays when the road is closed to car traffic.

Rome's fountain tradition goes beyond decoration. The city has over 2,500 public drinking fountains, known locally as nasoni because of their curved spout that resembles a large nose. The water comes from ancient aqueducts and is perfectly safe and refreshing to drink, saving you money on bottled water throughout your trip.

For a view of the entire city, climb to the top of the Gianicolo hill at sunset. From here you can see the dome of St. Peter's, the rooftops of Trastevere, and the distant Alban Hills. A cannon is fired from the hill every day at noon, a tradition dating back to 1847 that still startles unsuspecting visitors.

Day trips from Rome are easy and worthwhile. Tivoli, about 30 kilometers east, is home to the stunning Villa d'Este with its hundreds of Renaissance fountains and the ancient ruins of Hadrian's Villa. Ostia Antica, Rome's ancient port city, offers remarkably well-preserved ruins with far fewer crowds than Pompeii.

Before leaving Rome, toss a coin into the Trevi Fountain with your right hand over your left shoulder. Legend says this guarantees your return to the Eternal City. The fountain collects roughly 3,000 euros in coins each day, all of which are donated to a charitable organization that supports those in need.
